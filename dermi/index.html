Skin Disease Detection on Dark Skin

Background

Skin diseases are responsible for a large number of deaths in the United States, and the severity of these diseases are exacerbated the later they are diagnosed. People with dark skin are more likely to have their skin disease detected at a stage when it is more severe due to both a preconception that having darker skin prevents severe skin problems and, sometimes, due to a lack of access to healthcare.

        Over the past several years, image classifiers have proven increasingly capable in classifying skin diseases. However, datasets used for skin disease detection are primarily composed of images of skin lesions on light skins. Recently, in 2021, a group of researchers released the Fitzpatrick 17k dataset, composed of annotated images from across skin tones. Although the size of the dataset is insufficiently small for developing image classifiers for most sub-classifications, especially for images of dark skin, the dataset contains many hundreds of images of lesions on dark skin for the highest-level classifications: malignant, benign, and non-neoplastic. In addition, there is a smaller dataset of images that has approximately 500 images split by classification (malignant or benign) and Fitzpatrick scales placed into three groups of 1 and 2, 3 and 4, and 5 and 6, known as the Diverse Dermatological Images Dataset.

        Most image classification models developed to diagnose skin diseases are trained on datasets that primarily include images of people with light skin. As a result, when tested on diagnosing individuals with darker skin, these models show lower accuracy. This has been more thoroughly tested by the Diverse Dermatological Images paper, which compares the accuracy of several models and the accuracy of a dermatologist ensemble on images of skin diseases on dark skin. The paper shows that all existing image classification models used to classify skin diseases present much lower accuracies on images of diseases on dark skin in comparison to images of disease on light skin.

        Existing attempts to create fair image classification models are primarily based on debiasing image classification models through various methods. One method focuses on pruning parts of the model that account for features that are only present for or helpful for classifying either dark skin or light skin individually. In other words, it emphasizes maintaining neurons that account for features that distinguish between benign and malignant lesions across skin tones. Another focuses on preprocessing images by segmenting the skin lesion out of the image in question. Two models are trained and used: one that segments the lesion itself, removing miscellaneous background, and another one that classifies this highlighted region.

Each existing approach focuses on creating a singular model or set of models that can classify images of skin disease on both light and dark skin. However, there have been no approaches that have attempted to segment datasets by skin tone. This is particularly significant because the same diseases on lighter and darker skin look significantly different. Therefore, creating separate models for smaller ranges of skin tones could serve to improve accuracy significantly.
